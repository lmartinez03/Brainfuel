## 1. Categories Shipped

### 1.1 Memory
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/memory.ts` |
| Pool size | 30 curated questions |
| Generation | Hand-authored, curated bank |

**What it tests:**
Working memory is the ability to hold and manipulate information in mind over a short window of time. Memory challenges in Brainfuel include: digit span recall, narrative recall, paired-associate learning, mental sequence tracking, and categorical counting within held lists.

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Baddeley & Hitch (1974) proposed the multi-component model of working memory: a central executive, phonological loop, and visuospatial sketchpad. Our tasks target all three components.
- Jaeggi et al. (2008, PNAS) showed that training working memory with dual n-back tasks produced transfer to fluid intelligence measures. While the extent of transfer is debated, engaging the working memory system is well-established as cognitively demanding.
- Klingberg (2010, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*) reviewed evidence that working memory training activates the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and parietal regions, the same circuits implicated in executive control and impulse resistance.
- The "backward digit span" task (e.g. question mem-028) is used in clinical cognitive assessment (WAIS-IV) to measure working memory capacity and is sensitive to executive function deficits.

**Why this serves Brainfuel's mission:**
A user who must hold a 7-item sequence in mind before they can open Instagram cannot mindlessly tap; their attentional system is already occupied. This breaks the automatic/habitual loop.

### 1.2 Mental Math
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/math.ts` |
| Pool size | **Effectively unlimited** (procedurally generated) |
| Generation | Algorithmic: `generateMathQuestions(count, sessionSeed)` |

**What it tests:**
Three difficulty tiers: (Easy) addition, subtraction, and times-tables; (Medium) two-step calculations, percentages, squares, division; (Hard) three-step calculations, powers of two, reverse percentages.

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Dehaene et al. (2003, *Cognitive Neuropsychology*) identified the intraparietal sulcus (IPS) as the core neural substrate for numerical magnitude processing, activated during all forms of mental arithmetic.
- Multi-step arithmetic specifically loads the phonological loop (subvocalising intermediate results) and the central executive (maintaining the problem state) simultaneously, making it a dual-task that maximises working memory engagement.
- Butterworth (1999, "The Mathematical Brain") notes that mental arithmetic fluency is sensitive to practice and shows measurable improvement over short timescales ; unlike crystallised knowledge, it responds to effortful training.
- A seeded Mulberry32 PRNG is used, ensuring questions are reproducible within a session but vary across sessions. No two quiz sessions will draw the same generated sequence.

**Technical note:**
The generator uses parameterised templates (add, subtract, multiply, divide, percentage, square, power-of-two, reverse percentage) with randomised operands. Distractors are generated by offsetting the correct answer by small and varied amounts, making them plausible but wrong.

### 1.3 Puzzles
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/puzzles.ts` |
| Pool size | 32 curated questions |
| Generation | Hand-authored, curated bank |

**What it tests:**
Logical deduction (syllogisms, transitivity), odd-one-out classification, spatial reasoning (cube dissection, clock angles, 3D rotation), constraint satisfaction (river-crossing, switch/bulb), analogy completion, cryptographic substitution, and matrix pattern reasoning.

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Raven's Progressive Matrices (1936), the gold-standard measure of fluid intelligence, are fundamentally matrix pattern-completion tasks. Several Brainfuel puzzle questions (puz-017) directly replicate this format.
- Kane & Engle (2002, *Psychological Review*) linked PFC-mediated inhibitory control to performance on logic problems. Users who successfully complete syllogisms must suppress the intuitive but incorrect response, which is exactly the inhibitory control needed to resist impulsive social-media opening.
- Uttal et al. (2013 meta-analysis, *Psychological Bulletin*, n > 10,000 participants) found that spatial reasoning training is highly malleable and shows transfer to STEM tasks. Spatial puzzles (cube-cutting, clock angles, 3D dissection) target this.
- Lateral thinking puzzle types (river-crossing, switch/bulb) require the solver to abandon a fixated interpretation, a process linked to right-hemisphere anterior temporal lobe activity (Bowden & Jung-Beeman, 2003).

### 1.4 Riddles
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/riddles.ts` |
| Pool size | 32 curated questions |
| Generation | Hand-authored, curated bank |

**What it tests:**
Lateral thinking (photographer/darkroom riddle, elevator/short woman, two-guards paradox), self-referential paradoxes, false-premise detection (rooster/egg, plane crash survivors), wordplay-based misdirection, and narrative reconstruction (story puzzles like the albatross riddle).

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Bowden & Jung-Beeman (2003, *PLOS Biology*) used fMRI and EEG to show that insight solutions ("aha" moments) correlate with a burst of gamma-wave activity in the right anterior temporal lobe, a region associated with integrating distant semantic associations. Riddles specifically elicit this "insight" mode of processing.
- Kahneman (2011, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*) popularised the System 1 / System 2 distinction: riddles exploit System 1 fast-pattern-matching (wrong) and force System 2 engagement (effortful, controlled). This is precisely the cognitive mode that resists impulsive behaviour.
- The "false premise" riddle category (roosters don't lay eggs; you don't bury survivors) requires metacognitive vigilance: monitoring one's own comprehension rather than blindly accepting stated premises. This is a transferable executive function.

### 1.5 Sequences
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/sequences.ts` |
| Pool size | 30 curated questions |
| Generation | Hand-authored, curated bank |

**What it tests:**
Arithmetic sequences (constant difference), geometric sequences (constant ratio), Fibonacci/tribonacci, factorial sequences, prime numbers, perfect squares/cubes, letter sequences (alphabet patterns, months, days, ordinal numbers), mixed alphanumeric patterns, and verbal/numerical analogies.

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Cattell (1963) defined fluid intelligence as the ability to solve novel problems by detecting patterns, independent of prior knowledge. Sequence completion tasks are among the purest measures of inductive reasoning (the generalisation from examples to rules).
- Blair (2006, *Developmental Science*) reviewed evidence that fluid intelligence is one of the most general predictors of academic and occupational outcomes, and that it responds to effortful practice in ways that crystallised knowledge does not.
- "Second-order difference" sequences (seq-016, seq-029) require detecting a pattern within the pattern, a meta-level induction that correlates strongly with abstract reasoning ability.
- The letter sequence tasks based on months/days/number-words (seq-016, seq-017, seq-018) involve category-specific knowledge combined with serial recall, crossing multiple memory systems.

### 1.6 Wordplay
| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| File | `src/games/categories/wordplay.ts` |
| Pool size | 30 curated questions |
| Generation | Hand-authored, curated bank |

**What it tests:**
Anagram solving (LISTEN/SILENT, ASTRONOMER/MOON STARER), homophone identification, contronyms (Janus words), prefix/root knowledge, compound word generation, phonological manipulation (letter rotation), vocabulary (loquacious, ephemeral), metalinguistic awareness (palindromes, pangrams, polysemy), and orthographic reasoning (BOOKKEEPER double letters, spelling patterns).

**Cognitive science rationale:**
- Gernsbacher & Kaschak (2003, *Annual Review of Psychology*) reviewed neuroimaging evidence for left-hemisphere perisylvian language network engagement during verbal reasoning tasks. Verbal fluency tasks (anagram solving, synonym generation) increase functional connectivity between frontal and temporal regions.
- Baddeley (2003, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) identified the phonological loop as the most studied component of working memory and a core predictor of reading ability, language acquisition, and attentional regulation. Phonological manipulation tasks (anagrams, letter rotation) directly engage this system.
- Metalinguistic awareness (knowing that language is a system with describable rules: palindromes, contronyms, pangrams) is associated with higher reading comprehension and verbal intelligence (Gombert, 1992).

## 2. Recommended Additional Categories (Future Sprints)

Based on the research surveyed, the following categories are strongly supported by cognitive science and are recommended for a future content sprint:

### 2.1 Spatial Reasoning
**Rationale:** Uttal et al. (2013) meta-analysis found spatial reasoning is highly malleable (effect size d = 0.47 across 217 studies, n > 10,000) and transfers to STEM performance. The Nature Human Behaviour study (2021, n = 17,648 children) found visuospatial training was the most effective type for improving mathematics. A dedicated spatial category with 2D net folding, mental rotation, path tracing, and mirror image tasks would be highly valuable.

**Suggested formats:** 2D net to 3D shape identification, mirror image matching (described), mental rotation (described), shortest-path estimation.

### 2.2 Attention / Vigilance
**Rationale:** Sustained attention and vigilance training is directly linked to the executive control deficits that make doom-scrolling so compelling (Smallwood & Schooler, 2015, *Psychological Bulletin*). Tasks requiring detection of rare targets in a stream of distractors (N-back analogs, odd-ball detection) are the most direct training for the failure mode Brainfuel addresses.

### 2.3 Verbal Analogies (Extended)
**Rationale:** Verbal analogy reasoning (A:B::C:?) is one of the most validated fluid intelligence measures. Expanding from simple antonym analogies to functional, causal, and part-whole analogies would increase difficulty range and cognitive coverage.

## 4. Legal & Licensing Status

### 4.1 What We Authored
All curated questions (memory, puzzles, riddles, sequences, wordplay) were written original by the Brainfuel backend team for this project. They are inspired by classical cognitive puzzle genres but are not copied from any external source.

**Specifically:**
- Memory questions are original scenarios using fictional names and invented data.
- Puzzle questions are new formulations of classical logical reasoning genres (syllogisms, river-crossing, spatial dissection). The underlying logical structures are mathematical, so they are not copyrightable. Our specific wordings are original.
- Riddle questions are either (a) original rewordings of ancient/public-domain folk riddles, or (b) fully original. The "albatross soup" riddle is a well-known lateral thinking puzzle with origins in the 1970s lateral thinking movement (de Bono). No single author holds copyright on the genre or the plot structure; our specific wording is original.
- Sequence questions use standard mathematical series (Fibonacci, primes, factorials). Mathematical facts are not copyrightable. Our question formulations are original.
- Wordplay questions draw on standard English vocabulary, common linguistic phenomena (palindromes, homophones, anagrams), and public-domain word knowledge. No licensed word lists (Scrabble dictionaries, copyrighted vocabulary databases) are used.

### 4.2 What to Avoid
- **Mensa puzzle books and IQ test banks**: These are commercially published with all rights reserved. Do not reproduce questions from Mensa International, Mensa Workout, or similar publications.
- **NYT Games (Wordle, Connections, Spelling Bee)**: NYT holds trademarks and copyrights on these game formats and specific word sets. Do not reproduce game mechanics with identical branding.
- **Lumosity / BrainHQ**: These companies hold patents on specific adaptive training paradigms and own the content in their question banks. Do not use their specific question text.
- **Published IQ/cognitive test instruments**: WAIS-IV, WISC, Raven's Matrices: the actual test items are protected. We can describe the *type* of task (matrix reasoning, backward digit span) without reproducing specific items.
- **Trivia databases (Open Trivia DB, Jservice)**: These are community-licensed (Creative Commons) and could be used for a general trivia category, but trivia is not cognitively stimulating by research standards; it measures crystallised knowledge, not fluid intelligence. Not recommended for Brainfuel.

### 4.3 Trademark Note
- "One sec" is a trademarked app name (one sec GmbH). Brainfuel should not describe itself as "the one sec clone" in marketing or metadata. Internal documentation (like this file) may reference it for concept explanation.
- The question generator code is proprietary to Brainfuel. The math generator (Mulberry32 PRNG) is a public-domain algorithm.
